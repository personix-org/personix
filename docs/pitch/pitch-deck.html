Why now, and never before.

Because today we have the technology.

The state cannot keep pace with technological change — least of all with the exponential AI revolution. Its first instinct is to regulate, to ban, to make the problem disappear. For some, that opening is a gift — a respectable cover for authoritarian drift.

The state is an old tool, out of breath. We need its modern version: decentralized, dynamic, efficient.

But the new tool must preserve our freedoms and hold society together. Change the incentives that drive its offices, and let them modernize themselves. Solidarity by design, not by force. If we pull it off, it will be a small miracle.

ii. Why now

How it works  ·  five primitives

The architecture.

Math anchors the protocol.

1.
DID layer.
Sovereign identities. The substrate everything else attaches to.
2.
Claims.
Structured statements about reality. Cryptographically signed, costly to write.
3.
Verifiers.
Algorithmically and randomly selected. Stake their own reputation on accuracy.
4.
Aggregation.
Competing services interpret raw data into scores and risk.
5.
Declared policy.
Every actor publishes the rules they play by, in advance. Consensus emerges from the social network itself — the community calibrates what “radical” means, not a central decree.
iii. Architecture

Why it can't be corrupted  ·  four forces

Incorruptibility.

Social incentives keep it honest.

1.
Unpredictable target.
Verifier selection is nondeterministic. You can't bribe in advance someone whose name nobody yet knows.
2.
Reputational leverage.
Reputation accrues slowly and collapses catastrophically. The bribe is never large enough.
3.
Public promise.
Declared rules turn private behavior into auditable commitment. Every breach is a publishable claim.
4.
Mesh topology.
Capturing a hierarchy costs one bribe. Capturing a mesh costs more than any attacker has.
iv. Incorruptibility
